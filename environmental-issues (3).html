Topic A

Environmental Health

A healthy environment supports the physical, mental, and social well-being of humans. Polluted air, water, or soil can trigger various diseases — both infectious and non-infectious. In Indonesia, environment-based diseases like diarrhea and acute respiratory infections (ISPA) consistently rank among the top illnesses in health clinics.

1. Disease Spread

Diseases can be transmitted through four main pathways:

🤝

Direct Contact

Through open skin wounds — pathogens enter the body directly from contact with infected surfaces or people.

💨

Air

Via droplets from coughs or sneezes containing viruses or bacteria — spread through the air we breathe.

💧

Water & Food

Through consuming contaminated water or food — one of the most common sources of illness worldwide.

🦟

Vectors

Via mosquito bites causing malaria and dengue fever (DBD) — mosquitoes act as carriers between hosts.

2. Clean Water Availability

Safe drinking water must meet physical (clear, odorless, tasteless), chemical, biological, and radioactive standards. A water source is considered safe if it is at least 10 meters from any waste or sewage.

4. Textile Waste & Fast Fashion

The fashion industry contributes ~10% of global carbon emissions (UNEP, 2023) — more than international aviation and shipping combined. Indonesia generates 2.3 million tons of textile waste annually. You can help by choosing slow fashion, upcycling old clothes, and donating instead of discarding.

Topic B

Addressing Climate Change

Global warming is the steady rise in Earth's average temperature, driven by increased greenhouse gases — CO₂, CH₄, and NO₂ — in the atmosphere. Since the Industrial Revolution, human activities have dramatically intensified this natural greenhouse effect.

1. Causes of Global Warming

🏭

Fossil Fuels

Burning coal & oil for vehicles, power plants, and industry — the largest source of CO₂ emissions.

🐄

Agriculture

Especially livestock farming — cattle alone account for 14.5% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

🌳

Deforestation

Clearing forests removes carbon sinks and releases stored CO₂ — accelerating warming significantly.

💻

Digital Activity

Servers and data centers consume massive amounts of electricity — the internet has a real carbon cost.

2. Effects of Global Warming

Rising sea levels from melting polar ice caps
Increased frequency and intensity of natural disasters
Prolonged droughts in some regions; heavier flooding in others
Extreme weather events and outbreak of certain diseases
Animals migrating to higher altitudes and latitudes

3. Digital Carbon Footprint

8–9 hrs

Topic C

Overcoming the Energy Crisis

Indonesia still heavily depends on fossil fuels (petroleum and coal) — which are non-renewable and steadily running out. Since 2004, Indonesia has become a net oil importer. An energy crisis occurs when consumption keeps rising while supplies are depleted.

Alternative Energy Sources

Alternative energy can replace fossil fuels while producing far fewer greenhouse gas emissions.

☀️
Solar

Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity — clean, abundant, and increasingly affordable.

⚠️ Limited to daytime & clear weather
💨
Wind

Turbines capture wind and generate electricity. Clean energy source with Indonesia developing PLTB infrastructure.

✅ Clean; Indonesia developing PLTB
🌋
Geothermal

Steam from Earth's core drives turbines (PLTPB). Indonesia has world-class geothermal potential.

✅ Indonesia has vast geothermal potential
🐄
Biogas

Fermentation of animal waste produces methane gas — turning agricultural waste into usable fuel.

♻️ Uses agricultural waste as fuel
🌾
Bioethanol

Fermentation of sugar/starch biomass produces fuel — lower carbon footprint, can be produced locally.

🌿 Lower carbon; can be produced locally
🗑️
Waste-to-Energy

Waste converted to electricity via pyrolysis or incineration — reduces landfill volume and emissions.

♻️ Reduces landfill volume & emissions
💡
Remember: Having alternative energy doesn't mean using it carelessly. Wise and conscious energy use is still essential — every kilowatt saved matters. Energy efficiency works hand-in-hand with renewable energy adoption.

Topic D

Improving Food Availability

Though Indonesia is an agricultural country, food security challenges remain. Food security depends not just on production, but also on fair distribution — as seen in Singapore, which has limited farmland but excellent food distribution.

16.9
MODERATE LEVEL

1. Challenges to Food Production

🌱 Soil fertility degradation — 95% of food is grown in soil; chemical fertilizers reduce its quality over time
🦗 Pests, diseases, and water crises — threatening crop yields and food security
🏗️ Land use changes — agricultural land converted for buildings or industry
🌡️ Climate change — disrupting growing seasons and rainfall patterns

2. Solutions at Every Level

LevelWhat Can Be Done
🏠 HomeGrow herbs, vegetables, and chilies at home (hydroponics or conventional); reduce food waste
🏫 School / CommunityCommunity gardens, school food programs, partnering with food banks
🏛️ GovernmentBuild irrigation; modernize farming; diversify local food sources; strengthen reserves

3. Food Waste

Food waste refers to edible food that is thrown away or uneaten. It is a serious issue — globally, 1.3 billion tons of food is wasted every year (FAO). In Indonesia, food waste causes economic losses of 551 trillion rupiah annually and produces methane gas, worsening climate change.

SDG 13 Sustainable Development Goal

ClimateAction

SDG 13 — Climate Action calls for urgent measures to combat climate change and its impacts. It directly connects to everything covered in this chapter: reducing greenhouse gas emissions, protecting our environment, transitioning to clean energy, and ensuring food security for all people.

🌡️

Limit global temperature rise to 1.5°C

🌊

Strengthen resilience to climate hazards

🏫

Improve education & awareness on climate

🤝

Integrate climate action into national policies

Key Targets of SDG 13
13.1

Strengthen resilience and adaptive capacity to climate-related hazards and natural disasters in all countries.

13.2

Integrate climate change measures into national policies, strategies, and planning at all levels of government.

13.3

Improve education, awareness-raising, and human and institutional capacity on climate change mitigation and adaptation.

13.b

Promote mechanisms for raising capacity for effective climate change planning in least developed countries, including youth and local communities.
